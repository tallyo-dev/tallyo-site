# How to Set Up a Chore Chart for Kids (Ages 6–12)

## Step 1: Pick the right chores (start small)

Most chore charts fail because they start with too many chores.

Start with:

- 1–2 daily chores
- 1 weekly chore

If you need ideas, use <a href="/chores-by-age/">chores by age</a> and pick chores your child can finish independently.

## Step 2: Make the chores repeatable (same time every day)

A chore chart becomes a routine when it happens at the same time.

Pick one "anchor moment," like:

- right after school
- before dinner
- after breakfast

If your child is overwhelmed, switch to a 10-minute <a href="/daily-chore-list-for-kids/">daily chore list</a> first.

## Step 3: Add parent approval (this prevents fights)

The easiest way to reduce arguments is to make chores count only after approval.

Approval helps because:

- kids can’t self-award rewards
- you avoid half-finished chores
- rules stay consistent

## Step 5: Choose rewards (screen time works great)

If you want a full menu of reward ideas and guardrails, see <a href="/reward-system-for-kids/">reward system for kids</a>.

Rewards should be:

- visible
- redeemable in small options
- capped (so you stay in control)

Great starter rewards:

- 10–20 minutes of <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a>
- pick the family movie
- dessert choice
- stay up 15 minutes later on Friday

If your family wants cash, keep it weekly and predictable. See <a href="/allowance-for-chores/">allowance for chores</a>.

## Step 6: Make the chart visible (paper or app)

Paper works for some families. Apps work better when you need approvals, points, streaks, and reward tracking.

If your biggest issue is consistency, the app-based version usually wins because it creates momentum.

## Step 7: Run a 7-day "reset week"

The first week is about consistency, not perfection.

- keep the daily list short
- do approvals at the same time each day
- celebrate small wins

After 7 days, you can add one new chore or adjust coin values.

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How many chores should be on a chore chart?</h3>
    <p>Start with 3–5 total chores. Most families do better with 1–2 daily chores and one weekly chore. A short list done consistently beats a long list nobody finishes.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should I put on a chore chart?</h3>
    <p>Use chores your child can actually finish independently. If you need ideas, start with <a href="/chores-by-age/">chores by age</a> and pick a few repeatable tasks.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How do I get my kid to follow a chore chart?</h3>
    <p>Make chores smaller, do them at the same time every day, and use parent approvals. Rewards help when they’re earned and capped — not renegotiated daily.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should screen time be part of a chore chart?</h3>
    <p>It can work very well when it’s redeemed in small blocks with clear limits. See <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a> for a simple system.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should kids get paid for chores?</h3>
    <p>You don’t have to pay cash for every chore. Many families keep baseline responsibilities expected and let kids earn coins/points for extra effort — redeemed for rewards or a small weekly payout.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Is a paper chore chart or an app better?</h3>
    <p>Paper can be a good start. Apps usually work better when you need approvals, points, and reward tracking. If consistency breaks down, the app-based approach often adds momentum.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
