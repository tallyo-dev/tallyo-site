## Daily chores checklist (starter — all ages)

These three take about 10 minutes and work as a baseline for ages 6–12:

- [ ] Room reset — toys, books, and clothes off the floor (5 min)
- [ ] After-meal reset — clear dishes, wipe table (3 min)
- [ ] Prep for tomorrow — backpack, water bottle, clothes (2 min)

For a full daily routine with timing, see <a href="/daily-chore-list-for-kids/">daily chore list for kids</a>.

## Checklists by age group

### Ages 6–8 — Daily checklist

- [ ] Put toys and books away
- [ ] Put dirty clothes in the hamper
- [ ] Help clear dishes after meals (light items)
- [ ] Wipe the table

**Weekly add-on:**
- [ ] Help feed a pet (with supervision)
- [ ] Put clean clothes away (simple items like socks, shirts)

### Ages 9–10 — Daily checklist

- [ ] 10-minute room tidy
- [ ] Clear and wipe the table after meals
- [ ] Pack school bag and water bottle

**Weekly add-ons:**
- [ ] Unload part of the dishwasher
- [ ] Fold simple laundry (towels, socks)
- [ ] Sweep a small area

### Ages 11–12 — Daily checklist

- [ ] Load the dishwasher after dinner
- [ ] 10-minute room reset
- [ ] Pack school bag

**Weekly add-ons:**
- [ ] Vacuum a room (rotating by day)
- [ ] Change bed sheets
- [ ] Clean bathroom sink and mirror
- [ ] Help make a simple lunch

For more age-specific ideas, see <a href="/chores-by-age/">chores by age (6–12)</a>.

## Weekly chores checklist (rotating)

Give each child one or two of these per week, rotating so no one gets stuck with the same chore forever:

- [ ] Vacuum a room
- [ ] Change bed sheets
- [ ] Bathroom sink + mirror
- [ ] Fold laundry (simple items)
- [ ] Full room reset
- [ ] Wipe down light switches and doorknobs
- [ ] Help with a simple meal

## Make the checklist work (3 rules)

A checklist only works if these three rules hold:

<ol class="article-steps">
  <li><strong>Same time every day.</strong> Pick an anchor moment — right after school or before dinner. When chores happen at the same time, they become routine instead of a daily negotiation.</li>
  <li><strong>Approval before credit.</strong> Chores count after a parent checks them. No approval, no coins. This prevents half-done chores and keeps expectations clear.</li>
  <li><strong>Earned rewards (capped).</strong> Connect the checklist to something kids care about. The most common: earned screen time in small blocks. Keep a daily cap so it doesn't spiral.</li>
</ol>

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How many chores should kids have?</h3>
    <p>Most families do better with 1–2 daily chores plus one weekly chore. A short list done consistently beats a long list nobody finishes. Start with the minimum and add chores once the habit is established.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should be on a checklist?</h3>
    <p>Pick chores your child can finish independently in under 10 minutes. Use the age-specific lists above, or see <a href="/chores-by-age/">chores by age</a> for a broader selection.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should a chore checklist include a reward?</h3>
    <p>It doesn't have to, but connecting checklist completion to earned rewards significantly improves consistency — especially for kids ages 6–10. Keep rewards simple, visible, and capped.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How do I get my child to follow the checklist without reminding them?</h3>
    <p>Post the checklist where they can see it, tie it to a consistent daily time, and use parent approvals. Once the routine is set, kids start checking in themselves — especially when there's something worth earning.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Is a printable checklist or an app better?</h3>
    <p>Both work. Printable checklists are easy to start and visible. App-based systems add approvals, coin tracking, and streaks — which often add momentum when paper charts fade.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
