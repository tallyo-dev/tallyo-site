## What is "earned screen time"?

Earned screen time means screen time is not automatic. It is redeemed like a reward:

- Kids do a chore (or a short chore list).
- A parent approves the result.
- The child earns coins.
- Coins can be spent on rewards like 10–20 minutes of screen time.

This is different from "punishment" or taking screen time away. It is a clear exchange that creates momentum.

## Why "time limits" alone often fail

Time limits are important, but they often fail for one reason: limits answer "how much," not "how to get there."

Common problems when you only set limits:

- Kids ask repeatedly because there is no path to "yes."
- Parents negotiate in the moment.
- Rules change day to day depending on mood.
- Screen time becomes the only motivator in the house.

Earned screen time works better because it creates a predictable routine. Kids know what to do to earn it, and parents do not have to invent incentives on the fly.

## A simple earned screen time system (3 rules)

<ol class="article-steps">
  <li><strong>Screen time is earned in small blocks.</strong> Don’t offer one huge reward. Small blocks keep it motivating and easier to manage.</li>
  <li><strong>Chores only count after approval.</strong> No approval, no coins. This keeps the system fair and prevents “half done” tasks.</li>
  <li><strong>Daily caps protect your family rules.</strong> Kids can earn screen time, but within limits you set (school nights vs weekends, etc.).</li>
</ol>

If you're starting from scratch, start with a simple [chore chart for kids](/chore-chart-for-kids/) and use earned screen time as the first reward.

- Set a <strong>daily cap</strong> (example: max 40 minutes on school days).

## How to avoid fights and loopholes (parent approvals)

Earned screen time fails when kids think they can "game" the system. Approvals are the solution.

Parent approvals help because:

- You can require chores to be done correctly (not halfway).
- Kids can’t self-award screen time.
- Rules stay consistent even when you’re busy.

<div class="article-callout">
  <div class="panel-header">Make it stick</div>
  <p>Pick one daily "anchor moment" for approvals: after breakfast, right after school, or before dinner. Consistency beats complexity.</p>
</div>

Screen time is a strong reward, but you do not want it to be the only one.

Good alternatives:

- Pick the family movie
- Dessert choice after dinner
- Stay up 15 minutes later on Friday
- Choose the weekend breakfast
- Family game night choice
- Extra reading time together
- Small toy or craft supply (saved coins)

A mix of rewards keeps motivation high without increasing screen time endlessly.

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Is earned screen time a good idea?</h3>
    <p>Yes, earned screen time can work well when it is simple and consistent. Kids get a clear path to “yes,” and parents avoid negotiating all day. The key is earning in small blocks, using approvals, and keeping daily caps.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How much screen time should a 7-, 9-, or 12-year-old have?</h3>
    <p>There is no single perfect number for every family. Many families do better with clear weekday limits and more flexible weekends. Earned screen time helps because it keeps the routine consistent while still letting you set caps that fit your rules.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should count toward screen time?</h3>
    <p>Use chores your child can actually finish independently. Ages 6–8 do simple tidy-up tasks, ages 9–10 can help with dishes and laundry, and ages 11–12 can handle more independent chores like vacuuming and changing sheets.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How do you enforce earned screen time consistently?</h3>
    <p>Choose a daily routine for approvals (right after school or before dinner), keep rewards in small blocks, and use a daily cap. Consistency matters more than the exact point system.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What if my child refuses chores?</h3>
    <p>Start with fewer chores and make them smaller. Use rewards they actually value, and keep your rules predictable. If a chore is too hard or unclear, simplify it and build confidence first.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should screen time be a reward or a right?</h3>
    <p>Every family decides this differently. Many families treat some screen time as allowed, and extra time as earned. Earned screen time works best when it adds structure without turning everything into constant bargaining.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
