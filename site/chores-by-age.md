## How to choose chores that work (any age)

Before the list, here’s the rule that makes chores actually happen:

- <strong>Small beats perfect.</strong> A "2-minute tidy" done daily beats a big weekend reset nobody finishes.
- <strong>Repeatable beats random.</strong> Kids build routine when chores happen at the same time.
- <strong>Approval beats arguing.</strong> Chores count after a parent checks them.

If you want a simple structure, start with a <a href="/chore-chart-for-kids/">chore chart for kids</a> and add earned rewards.

## The system that makes chores stick (3 rules)

If you want a full rewards framework (chores -> coins -> rewards), see <a href="/reward-system-for-kids/">reward system for kids</a>.

<ol class="article-steps">
  <li><strong>Keep the daily list short.</strong> 1–2 daily chores + 1 weekly chore is enough to start.</li>
  <li><strong>Chores count after approval.</strong> Approvals prevent loopholes and keep things fair.</li>
  <li><strong>Let kids earn rewards.</strong> Coins/points are simple. Rewards can be <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a>, experiences, or a small weekly payout.</li>
</ol>

<div class="article-callout">
  <div class="panel-header">Start here</div>
  <p>Pick one "anchor moment" for chores: right after school or before dinner. The same time every day removes 80% of the arguing.</p>
</div>

## Reward ideas (not just money)

If motivation is the biggest issue, rewards can help — as long as you don’t turn everything into bribery.

Good options:

- 10–20 minutes of earned screen time
- Pick the family movie
- Dessert choice
- Stay up 15 minutes later on Friday
- Choose weekend breakfast
- Small toy/craft supply (saved coins)

If your family prefers cash: keep it simple and predictable (weekly payout with a cap). See <a href="/allowance-for-chores/">allowance for chores</a>.

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should a 6-year-old do?</h3>
    <p>Simple tidy-up chores work best: toys in bins, clothes in the hamper, setting napkins, and helping with a pet with supervision. Keep it short and consistent.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should a 10-year-old do?</h3>
    <p>Many 10-year-olds can handle more independent chores like loading the dishwasher, vacuuming a small room, cleaning a sink and mirror, and helping with simple food prep.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How many chores should kids have per day?</h3>
    <p>Most families do better with 1–2 daily chores plus a weekly chore. A short list done consistently beats a long list nobody finishes.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should kids get paid for chores?</h3>
    <p>You don’t have to pay cash for every chore. Many families keep baseline responsibilities expected and use coins/points for extra effort — redeemed for rewards like earned screen time or a small weekly payout.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How do you get kids to do chores without fighting?</h3>
    <p>Make chores smaller, tie them to a consistent routine, and use parent approvals. Rewards help when they’re clear, capped, and earned — not renegotiated every day.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What is the best chore chart system?</h3>
    <p>The best system is simple: a short daily list, approvals, and earned rewards. Start with a <a href="/chore-chart-for-kids/">chore chart</a>, then add rewards like <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a>.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
