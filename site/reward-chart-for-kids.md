# Reward Chart for Kids (Ages 6–12)

<p class="lede">A reward chart works when it’s simple, visible, and consistent. This guide shows a practical reward chart for kids ages 6–12 that’s easy to run every day.</p>

## Printable reward chart vs app-based reward chart

Printable charts can work, but they often break down when:

- kids ignore the chart
- parents forget to update it
- rewards get negotiated in the moment

App-based systems tend to work better when you need approvals, points/coins, and consistent tracking.

If you want the full framework (chores -> approvals -> coins -> rewards), see <a href="/reward-system-for-kids/">reward system for kids</a>.

## Step 1: Pick the "earn" side (chores)

Start with a short list:

- 1–2 daily chores (use a <a href="/daily-chore-list-for-kids/">daily chore list</a>)
- 1 weekly chore

If you need ideas by age, use <a href="/chores-by-age/">chores by age</a>.

## Step 2: Add approvals (the rule that prevents fights)

Chores count after approval.

<div class="article-callout">
  <div class="panel-header">Rule</div>
  <p>No approval, no coins. This keeps rewards fair and stops "half done" chores from becoming arguments.</p>
</div>

## Step 4: Choose rewards (keep the menu small)

A short menu prevents constant negotiation.

Great reward ideas:

- 10–20 minutes of <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a> (or use a <a href="/screen-time-chart/">screen time chart</a>)
- pick the family movie
- dessert choice
- stay up 15 minutes later on Friday

If you want cash, keep it weekly and predictable. See <a href="/allowance-for-chores/">allowance for chores</a>.

## Step 5: Run a 7-day "reset week"

For the first 7 days:

- keep the daily list short
- do approvals at the same time every day
- celebrate small wins

After a week, add one new chore or adjust coin values.

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What is the best reward chart for kids?</h3>
    <p>The best reward chart is one your family can run consistently: a short daily list, parent approvals, and earned rewards. Simplicity matters more than lots of stickers or complex rules.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Are reward charts bribery?</h3>
    <p>Reward charts become bribery when you negotiate in the moment. A consistent reward system is different: kids know the rules ahead of time and rewards are earned through clear actions.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What rewards work best for kids?</h3>
    <p>Earned screen time, choosing the family movie, dessert choice, staying up later on Friday, and small toys/crafts are common options. A mix keeps motivation high.</p>
  </li>
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How do I make a reward chart that kids follow?</h3>
    <p>Keep the daily list short, do chores at the same time each day, use approvals, and redeem rewards in small blocks. If the system is too big, it won’t stick.</p>
  </li>
</ul>
