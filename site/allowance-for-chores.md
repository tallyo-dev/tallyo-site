## Should kids get allowance for chores?

It depends on your goal.

- If your goal is <strong>teaching responsibility</strong>, chores should exist even without money.
- If your goal is <strong>teaching money skills</strong>, tying some allowance to effort can work well.

Many families get the best results with a hybrid approach:

- <strong>Baseline responsibilities</strong> are expected (no pay).
- <strong>Extra effort chores</strong> earn coins/points that can be redeemed.

This keeps the house running without constant bargaining while still giving kids a clear way to earn.

## Why "paying per chore" can backfire

Paying per chore can create two common problems:

- Kids start asking, "How much do I get?" before they help.
- Parents start renegotiating prices when chores don’t get done.

The fix is to make the rules predictable:

- Coins/points are posted ahead of time.
- Chores only count after approval.
- Rewards have clear prices and limits.

## A simple allowance-for-chores system (3 steps)

<ol class="article-steps">
  <li><strong>Choose 3–5 repeatable chores.</strong> Start small: 1–2 daily chores and a couple weekly chores.</li>
  <li><strong>Assign coins/points (not dollars) first.</strong> Points are easier to manage, and you can decide later whether points convert to money or rewards.</li>
  <li><strong>Approve chores before anything is earned.</strong> This prevents "half done" work from becoming a fight.</li>
</ol>

## Points vs money: which works better?

For kids ages 6–12, points/coins usually work better because:

- You can reward effort without making everything about cash.
- You can include non-cash rewards (earned screen time, dessert, choosing dinner).
- You can set caps and keep it aligned with family rules.

Cash allowance can still be part of the system, but it works best when it’s <strong>limited and consistent</strong> (weekly payout, not constant micro-payments).

## How to set rewards (and avoid "bribery")

Rewards work best when they are:

- <strong>Visible</strong> (kids can see what they’re saving for)
- <strong>Redeemable in small options</strong> (so motivation stays high)
- <strong>Capped</strong> (so you stay in control)

Good reward ideas:

- 10–20 minutes of earned screen time
- Choose the family movie
- Dessert choice
- Stay up 15 minutes later on Friday
- Small toy/craft supply

If your family wants cash allowance, keep it simple:

- Weekly payout only
- Maximum weekly amount (cap)
- No renegotiation mid-week

Many families use a hybrid: a chore chart + coins for rewards like <a href="/earned-screen-time/">earned screen time</a>, and occasional cash payouts on a weekly schedule.

## FAQ

<ul class="faq-stack">
  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>How much allowance should you give for chores?</h3>
    <p>There is no single perfect number. A simple approach is a small weekly cap that fits your budget and your child’s age. Many families get better results by using points/coins for effort and redeeming them for rewards (including small cash payouts) on a consistent schedule.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Should you pay kids for chores?</h3>
    <p>You don’t have to pay cash for every chore. A common approach is to keep baseline responsibilities expected, and let kids earn rewards for extra effort. This avoids turning the whole house into a negotiation.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What chores should be paid?</h3>
    <p>Paid chores usually work best for optional or higher-effort tasks (vacuuming, deep cleaning a playroom, changing sheets). Basic daily expectations can stay unpaid so the system stays stable.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What if my child only does chores for money?</h3>
    <p>That usually means the system is too “per-chore” and negotiable. Switch to points/coins, add parent approvals, keep stable values, and separate baseline responsibilities from extra-earn chores.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>Is allowance better weekly or daily?</h3>
    <p>Weekly is usually easier. Daily payouts create constant negotiation. Weekly rewards keep expectations clear and reduce the number of “transactions” you have to manage.</p>
  </li>

  <li class="faq-card">
    <h3>What rewards work besides cash?</h3>
    <p>Earned screen time, choosing the family movie, dessert choice, staying up later on Friday, and small toys/crafts are common options. A mix of rewards keeps motivation high without increasing spending.</p>
  </li>
